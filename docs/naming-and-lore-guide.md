## 1. Compound Names

Many species combine two simple idea-words into a single name:

Examples:
- **Lanternback** (light + anatomy)
- **Wandergrub** (behavior + creature form)
- **Marrowfin** (body material + anatomy)
- **Thornbellow** (environment + sound)

These compounds should be intuitive and evocative.

---

## 2. Hyphenated Forms

Some names use a hyphen to emphasize their two-part nature:

Examples:
- **Moon-Pip**
- **Star-Moth**
- **Eye-Sprite**

Hyphens are used when:
- the two words each convey a clear meaning
- the rhythm feels better split
- the species name benefits from a little flair

Use sparinglyâ€”most names still work without one.

# ðŸŒ’ Lore Style Guidelines

Lore in this world is short, evocative, and reads like a note from an in-world researcher who is both enchanted and slightly confused by these creatures.

### Tone Guidelines
- Magical but not grandiose
- Whimsical but not silly
- Naturalistic but with subtle supernatural twists
- Comfortably eerie at times
- Written with warmth and curiosity

### Length
Most lore is **1â€“3 sentences**.

### Format
A lore line should ideally:
1. Describe the creatureâ€™s appearance or physical quirk
2. Mention its behavior or temperament
3. Offer a small magical or folkloric detail

Example formats:

> "Gentle creatures with soft ember-lit bodies that appear to lonely travelers. Hearthmares leave warm ash hoofprints believed to calm bad dreams."

> "Small soot-spirits that nest in warm spaces and leave smudges wherever they sleep. Sootlings are mischievous but affectionate once tamed."

> "Drift-Backs are quiet, wind-marked creatures whose swirling patterned shells leave soft trails in the air as they move."
